How to become a nurse

You can get into this career through:

  • a university course
  • an apprenticeship

University

You can do a degree in adult nursing approved by the Nursing & Midwifery Council.

Some degree courses let you study another area of nursing alongside adult nursing.

You may be able to join the second year of a nursing degree if you already have a degree in:

  • a health-related subject
  • psychology
  • life sciences
  • social work

Full-time courses usually take 3 years.

Entry requirements

You'll usually need:

  • 4 or 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), or equivalent, including English, maths and science
  • 2 or 3 A levels, including a science, or a level 3 diploma or access to higher education in health, science or nursing

Apprenticeship

You may be able to do a degree apprenticeship in nursing if you work in a healthcare setting like a hospital.

The apprenticeship takes around 4 years and is a mix of academic study and on-the-job training.

You must be supported by your employer to take this route.

Entry requirements

You'll usually need:

  • 4 or 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C) and A levels, or equivalent, for a degree apprenticeship

What it takes

Skills and knowledge

You'll need:

  • sensitivity and understanding
  • the ability to work well with others
  • a desire to help people
  • knowledge of psychology
  • to be thorough and pay attention to detail
  • customer service skills
  • the ability to accept criticism and work well under pressure
  • patience and the ability to remain calm in stressful situations
  • to be able to carry out basic tasks on a computer or hand-held device

A day in the life of a nurse

The National Careers Service spoke to a registered nurse about her work. She is employed by the NHS and currently works as a district nurse in a residential care home and sees several patients at their homes.

What does a day in your life look like?

8.45am: We have a team meeting with GPs, ambulance technician and community staff nurse for a monthly meeting to discuss some of the patients.

9.50am: I start my rounds now. Many of my patients are within walking distance. If my patients are asleep, I make a note to come back later and move on to the next thing.

10.30am: I drive to see a patient in a nearby village to change a patient’s dressing. Her stomach wound had become infected but it’s responding well to treatment. I then travel to a patient in a remote area who needs a B12 injection and can’t leave the house.

11.30am: I visit a patient and teach her how to change the dressings on her ulcerated legs.

12.00pm: I visit the local residential care home to see if any of the residents need treatment. Part of my role is made up adult social care, and my visits provide a seamless link between the health service and social care.

12.30pm: Quick lunch before heading back to the medical centre.

2pm: Mary deals with a referral from the local hospital, as they diagnosed MRSA during an admission screening. It’ll need to be treated with a full body wash.

2.30pm-5pm: I also have a backlog of emails to deal with and management issues relating to the nurses in my team. The patients will always come first but the staff come a close second.

What you'll do

Day-to-day tasks

In this role you could be:

  • taking temperatures, blood pressures and pulse rates
  • helping doctors with physical examinations
  • giving drugs and injections
  • cleaning and dressing wounds
  • setting up drips and blood transfusions
  • using medical equipment
  • checking patients' progress
  • working with doctors to decide what care to give
  • advising patients and their relatives

Working environment

You could work in an NHS or private hospital, at a health centre, at a hospice, at an adult care home, at a client's home or in a prison.

Your working environment may be physically and emotionally demanding.

You may need to wear a uniform.

Career path and progression

You could do more training and specialise to become a:

  • district or practice nurse
  • children’s nurse
  • mental health nurse
  • occupational health nurse
  • theatre nurse

You could move into management as a:

  • matron
  • director of nursing

With a master's, you could become an advanced nurse practitioner or clinical nurse specialist then a nurse consultant.

You may also want to consider other careers that use similar skills including:

  • healthcare assistant
  • midwife
  • paramedic
  • physician associate

You could also become self-employed or work overseas.
